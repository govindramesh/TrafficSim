Background

Michigan Left
A Michigan Left is a turn where instead of turning left, drivers must turn right and make a Uturn. Michigan Lefts reduce congestion, the amount of crashes, and waiting times for left-turning vehicles and through-traffic. In places where Michigan Lefts have been enforced, the amount of crashes have been reduced by 30 to 60 percent overall.

Left Turns and Self-Driving Cars
Left turns are very complicated and involve making a lot of decisions. As a driver approaches an intersection, they must take in information such as how much time there is to turn, how many cars are in the opposite lane, how much space there is, and if there are any pedestrians crossing. At a traffic pattern, there are either cars waiting to turn left, or cars in cross-traffic waiting for the cars that are turning left, which creates congestion, wastes time and gas, and is dangerous to pedestrians, drivers, and cross-traffic. The National Highway Institute Research Center reports that "nearly 75% of all access-related crashes [to and from driveways from the main highway] are left turns", while data from the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration shows that 53.1% of crossing-path crashes involve left turns while only 5.7% include right turns. UPS routes its 110,000 vehicles to avoid left turns whenever possible resulting in fewer accidents, less waiting time at intersections, and higher fuel efficiency.

When programming self-driving cars, making a safe left turn is one of the toughest problems. Depending on the traffic conditions, making a left turn can take a long time even for human drivers, which creates less time and fuel efficiency. For driverless cars to make a left turn, the technology and programming gets even more complex. However, making a right turn at an intersection is simple even for self-driving cars. A 2010 USDOT study shows that 22.2% of all accidents are caused by left turns, compared to 1.2% caused by right turns, so not only is it easier, it's much safer as well. Human drivers can process everything at an intersection and respond accordingly, but driverless cars don't know how to read signs from other drivers. Self-driving cars have many sensors that can precisely measure the speed and distance of other cars/pedestrians, but they can’t reliably predict what those cars/pedestrians are going to do next. They won’t try and go through traffic or pedestrians, but instead, they will just stop creating potential traffic jams or other accidents. Right turns don’t involve as much decision making as left turns do, and are much easier to execute for self-driving cars, which opens up Michigan Lefts as a possibility in the era of autonomous cars.

Rationale

As the idea of self-driving cars is slowly being brought to life, one of the most challenging problems is the procedure the car will take at a traffic intersection. Left turns are very complicated and involve making a lot of on-the-spot decisions based on indicators in the surrounding area. Even human drivers can sometimes have trouble processing all of the information and responding accordingly. In some situations, making a left turn can take an unreasonable amount of time, not only creating more traffic congestion, but also reducing fuel efficiency. The technology for driverless cars to consistently make a safe left turn is very complex and will take some time to perfect. On the other hand, making a right turn at an intersection is simple and easy to execute even for autonomous vehicles. Under which circumstances is a right turn then a U-turn (Michigan Left) more efficient than a standard left turn?

Research Question

I am trying to determine under which conditions a "Michigan Left" is more efficient than a standard left turn. In high-traffic situations, standard left turns can include a lot of waiting times and high accident risk, so I think that a Michigan Left will be better under these conditions because it is safer, more straightforward and involves less waiting time. The result of this project could help determine when and where Michigan Lefts should be utilized in the new era of self-driving cars.

Methods

Data Analysis
In order to determine under which circumstances a Michigan Left is more efficient than a standard left turn, my program assigns random(with set parameters) numbers to each variable, from the number of cars in each lane to the duration of the light cycle. This creates a very large amount of unique scenarios. My program outputs which turn takes less time for each scenario, as well as some basic - but vital - information about the scenario itself. This will help me analyze the data I acquire from running the program multiple times in order to determine what factors are the most influential in making a Michigan Left more efficient than a standard left turn.

Materials
I used Python to create a program that simulates a traffic pattern under many different conditions rather than testing at an actual intersection with real cars. My program automatically determines the times for each situation.

Procedure

  • Draw a traffic intersection and identify all variables that influence cars when making a standard left or Michigan Left.
  • Write out all of the necessary code in pseudocode including various functions and variables.
  • Translate the pseudocode to Python.
  • Run the program multiple times to get a wide variety of results.
  • Analyze the data.

Conclusions

By analyzing the data I collected using my program, I found the correlation between the different variables tested and whether a Michigan Left of Standard Left was better. While I did have variables for most of the things that influence a turn at a traffic intersection, I only used the most important variables to determine my conclusions. The variables I used were the total amount of cars present, how long the light cycle was, and which light was green when the tested car approached the intersection. I found that Michigan Lefts took less time in somewhat high traffic. Standard Lefts were exceedingly better when there were very few cars present, but fared worse in extremely high traffic. Michigan Lefts performed better as the total amount of cars at the intersection increased. When the light stayed green for a shorter amount of time, Michigan Lefts were better, but there wasn’t too much variation when the time increased. The biggest influence on whether a Michigan Left or Standard left was better was which light was green when the car approached the intersection.
